Hudson, Texas: "There were thoughtful phone calls and letters of support and respectful, dissenting viewpoints were also shared," Beyak's statement released Thursday said, adding that she has been involved "personally and politically" with First Nations people since 1964. (CBC News) "The senator has stated unequivocally, many times over the years, in noting certain positive aspects of residential schools, as also recorded in the Truth and Reconciliation Commission report, one can never excuse or minimize the suffering that victims have experienced," Beyak said in her statement, written in the third person. "I speak partly for the record, but mostly in memory of the kindly and well-intentioned men and women and their descendants — perhaps some of us here in this chamber — whose remarkable works, good deeds and historical tales in the residential schools go unacknowledged for the most part," she said during her initial address. In her first public remarks since CBC News reported her comments more than a week ago, Beyak said she has many Indigenous friends and those that read her speech "recognize her knowledge, compassion and expertise" on the First Nations file.
